PPO vs. EPO: What Tennessee's Self-Employed Need to Know

April 30, 2026 8 min read Denton Casey

PPO vs EPO in Tennessee — the difference affects which doctors you can see, whether you need referrals, and what happens when you need care outside your network. Here's what matters.

When people compare health insurance plans, they almost always lead with price. That's understandable — premium is the most visible number, and it affects your budget every single month. But for many Tennesseans, the decision that will matter most when they actually need care isn't how much they paid for the plan. It's which doctors and hospitals they can access, and what happens when you step outside that network.

This is where understanding the difference between PPO and EPO networks becomes essential.

What Is an EPO?

An EPO — Exclusive Provider Organization — is a network structure that restricts you to a defined group of providers. Unlike an HMO, an EPO generally doesn't require a referral to see a specialist. But the "exclusive" part is the catch: if you go outside the network, the plan typically pays nothing. Not a reduced amount — nothing.

EPOs are common on the ACA marketplace in Tennessee and can carry lower premiums than PPO options (when subsidized), which makes them attractive on the surface. But the network restriction is real, and most people don't fully understand it until they need care from a provider who isn't in the plan's directory.

If you're in Murfreesboro and your regular specialist is affiliated with a Nashville health system that's outside your EPO network, you're paying the full bill out of pocket — or finding a new doctor.

What Is a PPO?

A PPO — Preferred Provider Organization — gives you significantly more flexibility. You can see any in-network provider without a referral, and you typically have the option to go out of network at a higher cost share, rather than having no coverage at all.

A nationwide PPO takes this further: instead of a regional or state-specific network, your coverage follows you across the entire country. Whether you're seeing a provider in Franklin, Nashville, Smyrna, or traveling to a major medical center in another state, your in-network benefits apply.

For self-employed professionals, 1099 contractors, and small business owners across Middle Tennessee — people who often travel, work with multiple providers, and value not having their coverage dictated by geography — nationwide PPO access is frequently a non-negotiable feature.

The Key Difference: What Happens Outside the Network

This is where EPO and PPO plans diverge in ways that matter most when you actually need care.

With an EPO, out-of-network care is almost never covered except in a genuine emergency. That means if you need a second opinion from a specialist at Vanderbilt who isn't in your EPO's directory, or if you're traveling for work and need non-emergency care, you're on your own financially. It also means that during emergency care you are covered — but the minute you are deemed stable, out-of-network rules kick back in. Most people never realize this happens.

With a PPO, going out of network costs more, but it's still covered. You have a safety net even when your preferred provider isn't in the directory, and you're never forced to choose between the doctor you want and coverage you're paying for.

Why This Matters Specifically in Middle Tennessee

Many of the communities we serve — Franklin, Nolensville, Smyrna, College Grove, Murfreesboro, Nashville, Chapel Hill, and Shelbyville — don't have major hospital systems or specialty centers within their immediate boundaries. Residents regularly travel to Nashville's larger health systems for specialty care, surgical procedures, and second opinions.

If your EPO network doesn't include the Nashville facilities you'd realistically use, you may be uncovered for care that's completely routine for Middle Tennessee residents. A nationwide PPO network removes that uncertainty entirely — your coverage works wherever you need care, not just where your plan says you're allowed to go.

No Referrals — But That's Where EPO Advantages End

One area where EPOs do match PPOs: referrals. Unlike HMOs, most EPO plans let you see a specialist directly without going through a primary care gatekeeper first. That's a genuine benefit and worth noting.

But outside of that, the restrictions are significant. No out-of-network coverage, often narrower provider directories, and limited flexibility if your care needs take you outside the plan's defined network.

When an EPO Might Make Sense

There are genuine cases where an EPO is the right call. If you qualify for meaningful ACA subsidies, primarily use local providers for preventive and routine care, and are confident your preferred doctors and hospitals are in the plan's network, an EPO can offer solid coverage at a lower premium.

The key is making that decision consciously — with full awareness of which providers are included and what the out-of-network reality looks like — rather than defaulting to the lowest premium and finding out the hard way when you need a specialist.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a referral on an EPO plan in Tennessee?

Most EPO plans do not require a referral to see a specialist — that's one advantage they share with PPOs. However, you must stay within the EPO's network, or the visit won't be covered.

What happens if I need care outside my EPO network?

In most cases, the EPO pays nothing for out-of-network care outside of a true emergency. This is the most important distinction between an EPO and a PPO, and the one that catches people off guard most often.

Is a PPO always more expensive than an EPO in Tennessee?

Not necessarily. On medically underwritten private plans, a healthy individual may access a nationwide PPO at a competitive premium — particularly without ACA subsidies. The comparison depends on your health profile, income, and the specific plans available to you.

My doctor is in Franklin, Murfreesboro, or Nashville — will they be in-network?

This depends entirely on the specific plan. Before enrolling in any EPO, verify that your preferred providers are in the network directory. One provider gap can expose you to significant out-of-pocket costs. An independent agent can check provider participation before you apply.
