Health Insurance for Consultants and High-Income Self-Employed in Tennessee

July 21, 2026 6 min read Denton Casey

Independent consultants, fractional executives, agency owners, high-billing freelancers — Middle Tennessee has a growing class of professionals who left a W-2 job, kept the income, and lost the benefits desk that used to handle all of this. When you're earning well on your own, health insurance stops being a form you fill out at onboarding and becomes a line item you're responsible for pricing, defending, and renewing every year. And here's the part that catches a lot of high earners off guard: the lane most people are told to use may be the most expensive one for you.

This post walks through why that happens and how to think it through. For the commercial side-by-side, our above-subsidy coverage page covers the numbers in more depth.

The Subsidy Was Never Built for You

The ACA marketplace works well for a lot of people, and much of what makes it attractive is the premium subsidy. But that subsidy phases out as income rises, and above a certain household-income line it disappears entirely. Cross that line and you're paying the full, unsubsidized marketplace premium — the same community-rated price whether you run marathons or not. For a healthy professional in a strong income year, that full-price premium is often the moment the marketplace quietly becomes the expensive option instead of the obvious one. We break down that mechanic in how income affects your health insurance premium.

Full Price Changes the Math

Once no subsidy is in play, the comparison opens back up. A privately underwritten PPO prices on your individual health history rather than a community rate, so a healthy high earner who'd pay full freight on the marketplace frequently finds the private lane more competitive on premium — often with a broader network attached. That's not a promise; underwritten plans are medically underwritten, so approval and pricing depend on your health. But it reframes the question. Instead of "which marketplace plan is least bad," it becomes "which of my actual lanes wins on total cost." Our ACA vs. private comparison lays out that head-to-head.

Network Access Is Part of the Value

Consultants travel. If your work takes you across state lines — client sites, conferences, a second home — the size and reach of your network matters as much as the premium. Narrow-network plans can look cheap until you need care somewhere your plan doesn't reach. A nationwide PPO with no referral requirements tends to fit the way high-earning professionals actually live and work. It's worth weighing the network right alongside the deductible and out-of-pocket max, not as an afterthought — the same numbers we walk through in the four numbers that define your coverage.

Don't Forget the After-Tax Number

For many self-employed professionals, health insurance premiums may be deductible — which means the sticker price and the real, after-tax cost can be two different numbers. This is a conversation for your CPA, not your insurance agent, and the rules depend on how your business is structured. But it's worth raising, because a plan that looks expensive on paper can land differently once your accountant factors it in. The point isn't a tax strategy; it's that high earners should compare lanes on their true cost, not just the premium line.

You're Not Locked to the Calendar

The ACA marketplace runs on an annual open enrollment window, with special enrollment periods for qualifying life events like leaving a job or moving. The privately underwritten market works differently: for healthy professionals, those plans can typically be applied for year-round. If you just went out on your own mid-year, or you're staring at a renewal that jumped, you may not have to wait for a window to fix it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why don't I get a subsidy when I buy health insurance on the marketplace?

ACA marketplace subsidies phase out as household income rises. Above a certain threshold you pay the full, unsubsidized premium. For many consultants and high earners, that full-price marketplace premium is exactly what makes it worth comparing other lanes, because the subsidy that makes the ACA attractive for lower incomes simply isn't there for you.

Is private health insurance cheaper than the ACA marketplace for high earners?

It often can be for healthy people, but not always. Private medically underwritten PPO plans price on your individual health history rather than a community rate, so a healthy high earner paying full freight on the marketplace frequently finds the private lane more competitive. The only way to know is to compare your actual numbers side by side, which is what an independent review is for.

Can I deduct my health insurance premiums if I'm self-employed?

Many self-employed people are able to deduct health insurance premiums, but the rules depend on your business structure and tax situation, so this is a conversation for your CPA rather than your insurance agent. It's worth raising, because the after-tax cost of a plan can look different from the sticker premium once your accountant weighs in.

Do I have to wait for open enrollment to change plans?

Not necessarily. The ACA marketplace has an annual open enrollment window plus special enrollment periods triggered by qualifying life events. Private medically underwritten plans generally allow you to apply year-round, so if you're a healthy professional who missed the window, you may still have a lane open to you.
